GROUP 3: READINGS IN PHILIPPINE HISTORY

Digital Preservation Case - The Vanishing Programmers of the 1980s

Why are early Filipino programming contributions poorly documented?

The story of the Philippines' early computing pioneers is as much about loss as it is about innovation. Decades before programmers of the 1980s began shaping the nation's digital future, the foundations for record-keeping had already been fractured by war. During World War II, Manila endured the devastating Battle of Manila and the years of Japanese occupation. Libraries, university archives, government offices, and private collections burned or were bombed beyond recognition. According to historical accounts of libraries damaged in the war, the National Library in 1945 had about 733,000 volumes before the war; after, only about 36,600 remained. The University of the Philippines library had ~147,000 prewar volumes; only ~3,000 were recovered, mainly due to students/faculty returning borrowed books. (Wikipedia, 2025). This left an irreplaceable gap: the very archives that might have safeguarded the first experiments with computing and data processing were simply gone before the field could even take root.

On the other hand, early computing in the Philippines was constrained by limited access to hardware, software, storage media, and skilled personnel. The physical media used (punched cards, magnetic tapes, early hard disks) were fragile or prone to degradation, and proper preservation was rarely prioritized (History of Philippine Digitalization, 2021).

How do gaps in archives affect our tech memory?

Gaps in archives whether intentional,accidental, or due to technological obsolescence have a significant impact on our Technological Memory or Collective Memory of Technology. These Gaps can distort our understanding of how technologies evolved, obscure contributions from marginalized groups, and create difficulties in learning from past innovations or failures. (Chun, 2011).

For instance, when key documents, source code, or archives disappear, it becomes hard to trace how and why certain innovations emerged. This loss is compounded by biases in preservation: dominant narratives often overshadow the work of women, minorities, and contributors from less prominent regions. Older digital formats can also become unreadable without active preservation or emulation, while institutions may selectively save only materials that fit their preferred story, omitting controversial or unsuccessful projects. Together, these factors hinder future technologists and educators, forcing them to reinvent solutions, overlook valuable lessons, and face gaps in learning resources.

What can we learn by comparing Philippine programmer documentation to that of other nations?

Comparing Philippine programmer documentation with that of other countries highlights how historical disruption shaped the record. World War II damage and later political upheaval left many Filipino archives thin, unlike nations that rebuilt and funded technical libraries after crises. Policy and funding also drive the strength of records. Countries with strong state or industry support. Such as the United States, Japan, or Germany kept formal reports and code archives. The Philippines lacked similar incentives, so many local innovations went unrecorded. Cultural attitudes toward preservation further explain the gap. In the Philippines, reliance on informal, oral knowledge without systematic digitization meant more information was lost compared with places that treated computing achievements as national milestones. Finally, bias compounds these gaps. Gender and regional biases exist everywhere, but they are harder to correct when the original documentation is already sparse. Overall, stable institutions, sustained investment, and proactive recovery projects are essential to safeguard the Philippines' early computing history.

Which digital preservation methods can help recover this past?

Recovering the history of early Filipino programmers requires a blend of complementary digital-preservation methods that address the political, economic, and gender biases that once dictated whose work was recorded. Digitization is a first step: scanning project notes, manuals, early source-code printouts, photographs of computing labs, and newsletters from universities or user groups converts fragile materials into durable, open formats such as PDF/A or TIFF. This not only prevents further physical deterioration but also makes records held by Manila-based or elite institutions accessible to a wider audience. Equally important is oral history collection. Interviewing surviving programmers, teachers, and community members from the 1970s and 1980s. Especially women, rural innovators, and activists capturing memories that were never formally archived. These testimonies counter the effects of Martial Law era suppression and the economic exclusion of poorer regions by valuing lived experience as historical evidence. Finally, metadata recovery and reconstruction helps re-establish the technical context of vanished projects. Using digital-forensics tools to extract file headers, dates, authorship, and directory structures from old diskettes or backup tapes can reveal how software evolved and who contributed, restoring credit to programmers whose names were omitted or lost. Building these materials into community-based digital archives open repositories maintained by universities, tech collectives, or families ensuring that no single government or elite institution controls the narrative. Together, these strategies can rebuild a more inclusive and accurate record of the 1980s Filipino programming scene, preserving the voices that earlier biases erased.

How did political, economic, or gender biases influence whose records were kept?

Political bias under Ferdinand Marcos's dictatorship meant that archives were used for censorship and propaganda. Testimonies from political detainees and activists were suppressed or destroyed, while state projects were highlighted. After the 1986 People Power Revolution, the Aquino government focused on documenting Marcos-era corruption, but many grassroots accounts remained fragmented or inaccessible (Boudreau, 2004; Roces, 2012; Thompson, 1995). Economic bias reflected the nation's deep class divide. Wealthy, urban Filipinos had access to universities, media outlets, and computing resources that enabled them to publish and preserve their work, while rural innovators and poorer communities lacked the means to record their experiences. Historical narratives therefore emphasize elite perspectives (Balisacan, 1993; Balisacan & Hill, 2003). Gender bias favored men's political and technological roles and downplayed women's agency. Even with Cory Aquino's presidency, women were often depicted through domestic or symbolic roles, and female activists' contributions were scattered in informal networks rather than formal archives (Roces, 2012; Tadiar, 2004).
